Wh- words that are used as interjections are tagged with the appropriate tag from the above list (not as INTJ). Their function is indicated at the phrasal level; see Interjection phrase (INTJP).

Wh-adverb (WADV)

Wh-adverbs (HOW, WHEN, WHENCE, WHERE, WHITHER, WHY) are tagged WADV. WHEN can also be tagged P.

Wh-determiner (WD)

WHAT and (THE) WHICH are tagged WD when not acting as the head of a wh- noun phrase, and as WPRO otherwise. Note the difference in this regard between wh- words and ordinary determiners, which are tagged as determiners invariably. 
(WNP (WD what) (NS men))

(WNP (WNP (WD which) (N prophet))
     (CONJP (CONJ and)
            (NX (N law))))

(WNP (WPRO what)				← note WPRO
     (ADJP (ADJ else)))

WHAT immediately preceding a determiner is tagged as WD, parallel to cases with SUCH. 

(WNP (WD what) (D a) (N nightmare))

(NP (SUCH such) (D a) (N problem))

For the treatment of WHAT THE DEVIL and similar expressions, see INTJP.

Wh-pronoun (WPRO, WPRO$)

WPRO is assigned to WHO, to WHETHER in the meaning WHICH OF TWO, and to WHAT and (THE) WHICH when these are acting as the head of a wh- noun phrase. Other cases of WHAT and WHICH are tagged WD. Note the difference in this regard between wh- words and ordinary determiners, which are tagged as determiners invariably.

Wh-marker in indirect questions (WQ)

WHETHER and IF are tagged WQ when they introduce indirect questions. The treatment as WQ rather than as C is motivated by the co-occurrence of WHETHER and IF with THAT, at least in Middle English. 
I asked her if/WQ that/C they are coming.
